Do's - Best Practices

Always verify URLs:

Check the full URL before clicking, especially in emails and messages from unknown senders.

Use HTTPS websites:

Look for the padlock icon and "https://" in the address bar for secure connections.

Hover before clicking:

Hover links to see their actual destination in the status bar.

Keep software updated:

Maintain updated browsers and security software for maximum protection.

Use SafeLink:

Scan suspicious links with our tool before visiting them.

Don'ts - What to Avoid

Don't click shortened URLs blindly:

Services like bit.ly can hide malicious destinations.

Don't trust urgent messages:

Phishers create false urgency to bypass your caution.

Don't enter credentials on suspicious sites:

Always verify you're on the legitimate website.

Don't ignore browser warnings:

Your browser's security warnings are there for a reason.

Never download from untrusted links:

Malicious downloads can compromise your entire system.

Security Education

Understanding Phishing Attacks:

Phishing is a cyberattack where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information. These attacks often use fake websites that look identical to real ones, tricking users into entering passwords, credit card numbers, and other sensitive data.

How URL Manipulation Works:

Cybercriminals use various techniques to disguise malicious links, including domain spoofing (e.g., goog1e.com), suspicious subdomains (e.g. pay-paypal.com), and URL shortening to hide destinations. Always be cautious of redirected links.

The Anatomy of a Suspicious URL:

Red flags include unusual character placement, excessive subdomains, urgent language or surrounding text, and brand names followed by suspicious extensions. If something looks or feels wrong, trust your instincts and investigate.

Safe Browsing Habits:

Develop good security habits by typing known URLs directly into the browser address bar instead of clicking links, bookmarking important sites, keeping browsers and security software updated, and always using secure connections.

QR Code and Mobile Threats:

QR codes can hide malicious URLs and bypass traditional email filters. Mobile users are particularly vulnerable as URLs are harder to inspect on small screens.
